CHAPTER 1 : Steward Band

Morning settled gently over Aramor, wrapping the mountain academy in
cool mist and the fragrance of cedar carried by the eastern wind. Bells
echoed through the valleys as Stewards gathered for another day of
formation, but among the senior Manifestation class there was a quiet
anticipation. Today was their final field trial, the last expedition they
would undertake before completing their years at the Academy.
Near the eastern gate, a small group of younger students had gathered
around the Young Steward. He never sought attention, yet it seemed to
follow him wherever he went.

One first-year studied a weathered map of the eastern vaults with growing
frustration. "I memorized every passage," he admitted, "but once we're
inside, everything looks the same."

The Young Steward rested a finger on the carved stone and smiled.
"The mountain isn't asking you to remember every turn," he said. "It's
asking you to notice what has changed."

The younger Steward frowned. "I don't understand."
"The map tells you where others have been." He tapped the carved trail
gently. "The mountain tells you where you are."

Before the student could ask another question, Senior Steward Kaelen
approached with a coil of climbing rope over one shoulder.

"You're doing it again."

The Young Steward looked up. "Doing what?"

"Answering simple questions with answers that make people think all
day."

A ripple of laughter spread through the group.
One of the first-years leaned toward Kaelen. "Does he always speak like
this?"

Kaelen pretended to consider the question.

"I've shared expeditions with him for six years."
"And?"
"I'm still translating."

Even the instructors inspecting supplies nearby smiled.
From the upper terrace, Master Steward Elarion watched the exchange.
The laughter warmed him, but his attention remained fixed on the Young
Steward after everyone else's had moved on. The students saw Aramor's
finest Steward preparing for his final trial. Elarion saw the longer pause
before each deep breath, the weariness hidden behind familiar composure,
and the quiet determination of someone who refused to let others carry his
burden. The healers had searched for years without finding an answer, and
with each passing season the old Master found himself wrestling with the
same painful truth: hope alone could not heal what no one understood.
When the laughter faded, Elarion descended the terrace and stepped before
the waiting expedition.

"The mountain has no interest in your reputation," Elarion said. "It reveals
only whether you have learned to listen. Walk together. Observe carefully.
Return together."

The Stewards bowed before setting out from the Academy.

The ground trembled.

The eastern trail wound beneath towering cedars where clear streams
crossed polished stone and morning birds greeted the rising sun.
Conversation came easily as the senior Stewards exchanged stories from
earlier expeditions while the younger students listened with the quiet
admiration reserved for those nearing graduation. For a little while, the
morning felt exactly as it should.

Once.

The feeling didn't last.

"Everyone away from the cliff!"

Near a narrow brook, the Young Steward gradually slowed until he
stopped altogether. His eyes remained fixed on the water flowing over the
smooth stones.

Years of training overcame surprise. The Stewards moved without panic,
pulling the younger students toward safer ground as Gifts awakened across
the trail. Walls of earth rose to intercept falling rock while currents of
wind swept choking dust away from the retreating formation. Commands
passed calmly from one Steward to another, each trusting the others to do
their part.

Kaelen was the first to notice. "Something wrong?"
The Young Steward crouched beside the stream without answering. Pale
ribbons of sediment drifted through the otherwise clear water. There had
been no rain for days. Above them, the ridge hawks that usually circled the
cliffs were nowhere to be seen, and even the forest had fallen strangely
quiet.

He dipped his fingers into the current, then looked toward the ridge.
"The mountain is changing."

Kaelen followed his gaze but saw nothing except trees swaying gently in
the morning breeze.

"Are you sure?"

Before the Young Steward could answer, a deep vibration rolled beneath
their feet.

Then a second time, violent enough to send loose stones skipping down
the slope.

Elarion reacted instantly.

The Young Steward did not watch the avalanche.
He watched the mountain.

Beneath the roar of collapsing stone, he sensed a pattern hidden within the
chaos. Ancient pillars still carried impossible weight. Buried chambers
shifted but had not yet failed. The mountain was breaking, but it was not
breaking everywhere.

His eyes found a narrow shelf beneath the eastern ridge.

"There!" he shouted.

Elarion did not hesitate.

"Move!"

The expedition surged toward the narrow shelf just moments before the
trail behind them disappeared beneath crashing stone. Ahead, the ancient
entrance to the eastern vault split apart, collapsing into a cloud of dust that
swallowed the mountainside.

For a heartbeat, no one moved.

Then a faint cry rose from beneath the rubble.

"Help..."

Kaelen lunged toward the collapse, but the Young Steward caught his arm.

"If we dig there, we bury them."

The words cut through the panic. Around them, the mountain was still
settling. Fresh cracks raced across the cliff face, and loose stones
continued to tumble from the shattered entrance. The passage that had
trapped the students was now holding up part of the ridge itself.
The Young Steward dropped beside an old survey map while several
earth-sense Stewards knelt nearby, their hands pressed against the
trembling ground. His eyes moved over faded tunnel markings before
closing as he followed the shifting pressure beneath the mountain. After a
long moment, he opened them and pointed toward a forgotten passage
near the edge of the map.

"There."

An instructor looked where he was pointing and frowned.

"That tunnel was sealed generations ago."

"It was," the Young Steward said. "The earthquake opened it."

No one wasted another moment. Earth Stewards secured the unstable slope
while wind wielders cleared the choking dust from the newly revealed
entrance. Kaelen led the first team into the narrow passage with the Young
Steward close behind, following directions that seemed less like guesses
than careful conversations with the mountain itself.

They found the trapped students alive beneath a pocket of stone no larger
than a room. One careless movement would have brought the ceiling down
upon them, so the rescue became an exercise in patience rather than
strength. Guided by the Young Steward, the Stewards removed only the
stones the mountain was willing to surrender. Minutes later, the last of the
trapped students emerged into the morning light to the relieved cheers of
those waiting outside.

Only then did the Young Steward falter. He steadied himself with a slow breath
and straightened before anyone could step toward him. To the others, it was
nothing more than a moment's fatigue after a difficult rescue. Elarion knew
better.

Relief crossed the old Master's face as the rescued students embraced their
companions, but it lasted only a moment before concern quietly returned.
Around them, the Stewards celebrated a rescue that would be remembered
for years. Only Elarion noticed the price it had demanded from the young
man who had led it.

The journey back to Aramor was far quieter than the one that had begun
that morning. News of the rescue reached the Academy before they did,
and by the time the expedition emerged from the forest, students had
already gathered along the terraces overlooking the eastern gate. The
moment the rescued Stewards came into view, anxious silence gave way
to relieved applause that echoed across the courtyard.

The younger students rushed forward, surrounding the survivors with
relieved laughter and a flood of questions. Instructors embraced those they
had feared lost, while healers led the injured toward the infirmary.

Although every Steward had shared in the rescue, grateful eyes inevitably
settled on the Young Steward.

One instructor approached and bowed.

"Thank you. You brought them home."

The Young Steward returned the gesture with quiet respect.

"We all did."

The instructor smiled.

"No. You reminded them how."

Before another word could be spoken, a familiar Guardian Beast emerged
from the cedar grove overlooking the courtyard. The great silver-furred
creature crossed the open square without hesitation and lowered its
massive head against the Young Steward's shoulder.

Conversations faded.

Guardian Beasts were fiercely discerning. They did not offer affection
lightly, nor without reason.

The Young Steward smiled and rested a hand against the creature's neck.
"You worried too?"

The beast answered with a low rumble before turning and disappearing
among the cedars as quietly as it had come.

The courtyard slowly returned to life, but the Elders remained where they
stood. While everyone else celebrated, they noticed the Young Steward's

face had grown noticeably paler since the rescue. He folded his hands
behind his back, hiding the faint tremor in his left hand so naturally that
only those who knew him best would have recognized it.

Elarion recognized it immediately.

His eyes lingered on the Young Steward for a moment before he looked
away. There would be time to speak later. Today belonged to the students.
As the excitement gradually settled, one of the senior instructors called the
graduating class together. It was time for the final tradition before
commencement.

The northern terrace overlooked the oldest corner of Aramor, where the
Ancient Cedar had stood since before the Academy's first stone was laid.
Every graduating Manifestation Steward came there to perform one final
act of stewardship—not as a test of power, but as a reminder that every
Gift existed first to preserve life.

This year, however, the ceremony began in uneasy silence.
One great branch of the cedar had withered. Its leaves had browned
despite weeks of careful tending, and the Academy's gardeners could do
nothing but watch as life slowly withdrew from the ancient tree.
The Young Steward stepped forward and rested one hand against the
weathered bark.

Closing his eyes, he let the urgency of the morning fall away. His
Manifestation flowed quietly into the ancient cedar, almost unseen, like
water reaching thirsty roots. A soft emerald glow spread beneath the bark,
climbed through the trunk, and disappeared into every branch. Before the
watching students, brittle leaves deepened to green, fresh buds unfolded
along the dying limb, and the weary tree seemed to draw its first full
breath in many seasons.

For a moment, the terrace remained silent as the restored cedar swayed
gently in the morning breeze. Then applause rose from every corner of the
gathering. One of the younger students whispered in awe, "Perfect
Manifestation..."

The Young Steward simply smiled and stepped aside, making no attempt
to receive the praise. Before long, every eye had turned from him to the
ancient cedar, exactly as he had hoped.

As the crowd gathered around the ancient tree, Elarion noticed him place a
hand against the terrace railing. The movement was small enough to
escape everyone else's attention. A single breath later he straightened, his
familiar composure returning before anyone thought to look twice.
By evening, the Academy assembled in the Hall of Stewards.

Lantern light warmed the cedar pillars as the graduating class stood before
the Assembly of Elders. Families, instructors, and younger Stewards filled
the galleries in respectful silence while the final ceremony began with the
same simplicity that had marked Aramor for generations. One by one, the
graduates received their instructors' blessing before returning quietly to
their places.

When the last Steward stepped back into line, Elarion remained at the
center of the hall.

"There are moments," he said, "when an Academy is privileged to
recognize not merely what a Steward has accomplished, but who that
Steward has become."

An Elder stepped forward carrying a small cedar case. As the lid was
lifted, a quiet murmur spread through the hall. Resting upon dark velvet
lay a woven band of white and deep green threads, fastened by a silver
clasp engraved with the crest of Aramor.

Few living Stewards had ever seen the Steward's Band bestowed.
Elarion received it from the Elder with both hands before turning toward
the Young Steward.

"From the day you first entered these mountains," Elarion said, "you have
chosen understanding before recognition, compassion before praise, and
faithfulness before ambition. More than the strength of your Gift, it is your
character that has shaped this Academy."

The Young Steward stepped forward, but the moment his gaze fell upon the
Steward's Band, he paused. It lasted only a heartbeat before he reached for it, too
brief for most to notice. The students saw humility. Elarion and Kaelen saw
something else.

They understood what the others could not. The Band represented a
lifetime of service, and for the first time since they had met him, the
Young Steward seemed uncertain whether that lifetime still belonged to
him.

Elarion met his eyes and answered with the faintest of smiles. Without another
word, the Young Steward received the Band with both hands, fastened it around
his wrist, and bowed deeply before the Assembly. For a brief moment the hall
remained perfectly still, as though everyone wished to hold the moment a little
longer. Then warm applause filled the chamber.

It was not the thunderous acclaim given to victorious generals or
celebrated champions. It was warmer than that. Teachers who had watched
him grow smiled openly. Younger students applauded until their hands

reddened. Even the Elders, who rarely displayed emotion during
ceremony, could not entirely hide their pride.

When the applause finally quieted, the Young Steward looked around the
hall with visible humility.

"Whatever good you have seen in me," he said, "first grew here. It belongs
to the teachers who formed me, the companions who walked beside me,
and the truth that guided us all."

He bowed once more before returning to his place.

The applause resumed, but Elarion remained still. He watched the Young
Steward standing beneath the lanterns, quietly committing the moment to
memory. For reasons he could not explain, the old Master found himself
wondering if he was witnessing not the end of a student's journey, but the
last evening before Aramor lost one of its own.

The celebration continued below the Hall of Stewards, but before the
lanterns had all been lit, the Young Steward slipped quietly away. His feet
carried him along the familiar western path until the Academy disappeared
behind the cedars and the mountains opened before him beneath the fading
sky. He had stood at this overlook countless times over the years, yet the
valley below had never felt so difficult to leave.

Elarion was already there, resting one hand on the stone rail. He did not
turn as the footsteps approached.

"I thought you might come here."

The Young Steward smiled faintly. "I thought you might be waiting."
For a time they stood together without speaking. The laughter drifting up
from the Academy sounded distant now, carried on the evening breeze

with the scent of cedar and fresh rain. It should have been a night of
celebration. Instead, both men watched the last light fade beyond the
mountains, neither willing to disturb the silence that had always served
them better than hurried words.

For a time, they stood together without speaking, watching the last light
fade beyond the mountains. At length, Elarion turned to him.
"You've decided."

The Young Steward rested his hands on the stone railing and nodded.

"Yes."

"I had hoped today would change your mind."

"It did." He looked down at the woven Steward's Band encircling his
wrist, his thumb absently tracing its threads. "The rescue reminded me
how much I still want to serve. It also reminded me that one day I may not
be able to."

Elarion's expression hardened with quiet resolve. "We are still searching.
The healers have not given up, and neither has your friend. Every season
he brings another theory, another possibility. Stay a little longer. Let us
keep searching with you."

"I know they haven't given up." The Young Steward's voice remained
calm, but beneath it lay a weariness he could no longer conceal. "That's
exactly why I have to leave."

The old Master frowned.

"I cannot keep asking all of you to spend your lives searching for an
answer that may not exist." He looked back toward the Academy where
warm lanterns now glowed among the cedars. "You taught me that a
Steward does not live for himself. I can't ask all of you to spend your lives
trying to save mine."

When he finally looked at his student again, there was no trace of
argument left in his eyes.

The words struck Elarion more deeply than any argument could have.

"If you must go," he said quietly, "then go with my blessing, not because
you have lost faith in what we taught you, but because you still believe the
truth is worth seeking."

For years he had prepared himself for this conversation. He had gathered
reasons to stay, promises of hope, reminders of the Founding Truth.
Standing beside the young man he had raised, none of those words seemed
equal to the burden before them.

At last he spoke the only one that still mattered.

"Stay."

The Young Steward closed his eyes.

"I want to."

The answer came so softly that Elarion almost wished he had not heard it.
"I want to walk these mountains for the rest of my life. I want to teach
those first-years who still think every map is the mountain itself. I want to
grow old here." A quiet smile crossed his face before fading into sadness.
"But every morning I wake wondering whether I have less time than I did
yesterday. If there is another path, I have to find it while I still can."
Neither man spoke after that.

The evening breeze stirred the cedars below, and somewhere in the
distance the Academy bells marked the end of another day. Elarion
listened to their familiar sound and realized, perhaps for the first time, that
Aramor could not protect everyone it loved.

The Young Steward met his gaze and nodded.

"I will."

Elarion stepped forward and embraced him. For a long moment neither of
them spoke. It was no longer the embrace of a Master and his student, but
of a father who understood that love sometimes meant letting go. When
they finally stepped apart, Elarion rested a hand on the Steward's Band
around the young man's wrist.

"Wherever the road leads," he said, "remember this. The Band is not what
made you a Steward. It only reminds others of what you have always
been."

The Young Steward lowered his eyes to the woven threads around his
wrist, turning them gently between his fingers as though committing their
weight to memory. After a long silence, he finally looked up and nodded.

"I promise."

Elarion searched his face for a long moment before returning the nod. He
believed the promise. What troubled him was not the young man's
sincerity, but the burden he would one day carry in trying to keep it.
They stood together a little while longer, saying nothing. There were no
words left that could ease what lay before them.

It was only later that night, long after they had parted, that the Young
Steward made the decision to return the Steward's Band.

He looked at the words already written across the page, then lowered the
pen once more and added a final line.

That night, long after the celebration had faded and Aramor lay asleep
beneath the mountains, the Young Steward returned alone to the western
overlook. Moonlight caught the woven threads of the Steward's Band as he
opened the worn leather journal that had accompanied him through every
year at the Academy.

There must be another path.

He turned to the final blank page.

For several moments the pen remained motionless above the paper. At
last, he began to write.

The healers failed again.

My friend still believes the connection can be restored.

Morning came without ceremony.

Before the Academy had fully awakened, the Young Steward entered the
Hall of Stewards. Only Elarion and the Elders waited inside. No one
seemed surprised to see him. Whether they had expected this morning or
merely feared it, he could not tell.

He walked to the cedar pedestal and rested his hand upon the Steward's
Band. For a moment he simply looked at it, remembering the applause, the
smiles, and the quiet pride in Kaelen's face only hours before. Then he
slipped it from his wrist and laid it gently inside the open cedar case.

Elarion wants me to wait.

"I cannot wear this," he said softly. "Not while my path is leading me
away from the one it represents."

The pen stopped.

Sorrow crossed Elarion's face, though his voice remained steady.

His eyes drifted toward the Academy below. Lanterns still glowed among
the cedars, and every familiar path seemed touched by memory. He
remembered crossing the stone bridges as a frightened boy, studying
beneath the bell towers, laughing with Kaelen after failed exercises, and
sitting beside Elarion at the western overlook while the mountains quietly
answered questions too large for words.

"The Band never made you a Steward."

Aramor had taught him how to listen, how to serve, and how to become
more than the Gift entrusted to him. Yet for all it had given him, it could
not save him.

"No," the Young Steward replied. His eyes lingered on the woven threads
before he slowly closed the case. "But today it asks me to represent a
future I cannot promise."

Silence settled over the hall.

The Elders offered no protest. Perhaps each of them understood that there
are moments when love must release what it cannot protect.

The Young Steward bowed deeply to them, then turned to Elarion. Neither
reached for the other. They had already said everything that mattered the
night before.

As the great cedar doors opened, the first light of dawn spilled across the
stone floor and stretched toward the threshold.

He crossed it without looking back.

Outside, Aramor was beginning to wake. Students crossed the courtyards
with journals tucked beneath their arms, unaware that one of their own
was leaving the only home he had ever known. Cedars swayed gently in
the morning breeze, and somewhere along the northern terrace, the ancient
tree he had restored whispered with new leaves.

The Young Steward passed through the eastern gate without looking back.
No voice called him to stay, and no footsteps followed as he took the road
beyond the mountain, carrying no Steward's Band, no certainty, and no
answer except the question that had become stronger than fear.

By sunset, Aramor could no longer see him.

Neither the Young Steward nor those he left behind knew that the path he
chose that morning would one day change Zionara forever.
